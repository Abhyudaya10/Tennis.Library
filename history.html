Tennis Times












Origins & Early Beginnings

The game of tennis has a long history, with roots reaching back to ancient civilizations. Although the exact origins are difficult to trace, it is widely believed that games involving balls and hitting motions were popular in ancient Egypt, Greece, and Rome. These early games shared basic concepts with tennis but were far less structured. The version most similar to modern tennis took shape in medieval France with a game called Jeu de Paume, or “game of the palm,” which emerged around the 12th century. In Jeu de Paume, players initially struck the ball with their hands, but as the game progressed, they began to use leather gloves and paddles to improve control. Eventually, these paddles evolved into the first rackets, setting the foundation for a more sophisticated sport. By the 16th century, Jeu de Paume had transformed into "real tennis," which introduced enclosed courts, specialized rules, and scoring terms that remain in modern tennis, such as “love” and “deuce.” Real tennis grew in popularity among European nobility, particularly in England and France. In fact, King Henry VIII of England was an avid player and championed the game, helping to establish it as a royal pastime. Despite its popularity, real tennis remained largely an exclusive sport, limited to the aristocracy and played primarily in indoor courts. This version of the game laid down principles and scoring systems that would significantly influence the modern game of lawn tennis.

The Birth of Mordern Tennis

Modern tennis, often referred to as lawn tennis, was officially born in the 19th century and was popularized through the efforts of Major Walter Clopton Wingfield. In 1873, Wingfield created a game called “Sphairistikè” (Greek for “ball-playing”) and developed a version that could be played on a grass surface, which was more accessible and suited for outdoor play. This new adaptation took elements from real tennis but modified them to create a simpler, more recreational activity that could be enjoyed by the public. Lawn tennis rapidly gained popularity in Victorian Britain, where it appealed to both men and women as an elegant, social sport. In 1877, the All England Club, based in Wimbledon, codified a standardized set of rules and dimensions for the court and net, which they used for the first official Wimbledon Championship. This tournament, initially featuring only men’s singles, marked the birth of competitive modern tennis. As Wimbledon grew in prestige, other championships emerged, and lawn tennis started to take shape as an international sport. By the late 19th century, additional events, such as doubles matches and women’s competitions, were introduced, further expanding the sport’s reach and appeal. Lawn tennis thus evolved from a leisure activity into an organized competitive sport with established rules, which were soon adopted worldwide.

Expansion & Globalization

The popularity of tennis quickly spread beyond Britain to countries across Europe and North America. During the late 19th and early 20th centuries, tennis clubs began to emerge in these regions, and the sport became accessible to a broader range of social classes and communities. Unlike real tennis, which had been largely confined to royalty and aristocrats, lawn tennis found enthusiastic support from middle and working-class people, making it a truly international game. The founding of the International Lawn Tennis Federation (ILTF) in 1913, with representatives from 12 nations, marked a major milestone in tennis history. The ILTF was established to promote the sport globally, ensuring consistency in rules and standards across borders. This organization also played a crucial role in formalizing the Grand Slam tournaments. Wimbledon (established in 1877), the US Open (1881), the French Open (1891), and the Australian Open (1905) were already prominent tournaments, but they were united under the Grand Slam label in the 1920s, making them the pinnacle of competitive tennis. Each Grand Slam offered unique challenges due to differences in court surfaces: grass at Wimbledon, clay at the French Open, and hard courts at the US Open and Australian Open. These surface variations brought out diverse playing styles and strategies, adding layers of skill and versatility to the game. By the mid-20th century, tennis had become one of the world’s most widely played sports, with millions of recreational and competitive players, as well as growing media coverage of tournaments.

The Open Era

The year 1968 marked a transformative period in tennis history, ushering in what is known as the Open Era. Before this time, tennis tournaments were divided into separate amateur and professional circuits, which limited competition and created a distinct hierarchy in the sport. With the advent of the Open Era, professional players were allowed to compete alongside amateurs in all tournaments, including the prestigious Grand Slams. This decision immediately increased the level of competition and attracted a larger, more diverse group of players. It also allowed tournaments to offer substantial prize money, paving the way for tennis to become a lucrative career path. The Open Era coincided with the rise of television broadcasting, which played a major role in making tennis a global spectacle. Stars such as Billie Jean King, Rod Laver, Bjorn Borg, Martina Navratilova, and Arthur Ashe became household names, and their rivalries captivated audiences around the world. Technological advancements also contributed to the sport's evolution. Racket materials shifted from wood to aluminum, and then to lighter, more durable graphite, allowing players to hit with more power and precision. Changes in strategy, particularly the shift from serve-and-volley tactics to baseline-focused play, added complexity to the game. Sponsorships, endorsements, and branding transformed the professional circuit, making it more financially rewarding for players and raising the sport’s profile as a high-stakes international competition. The Open Era not only democratized tennis but also solidified it as one of the world’s most celebrated sports.
